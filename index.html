The question we asked

Singapore has built a strong missionary-sending culture. The infrastructure to welcome them home is largely invisible.

In 2025, Antioch21 and Asian Cross Cultural Training Institute (ACTI) asked 35 returned missionaries a simple but rarely-asked question: what was it actually like to come home? This brief summarises what they told us.

The people in this survey served for an average of 13 years — some for over 40. They returned to a Singapore that had moved on without them, often quietly and without ceremony, into churches and communities that had little framework for receiving them well.

A note on this data

This is a small, self-selected sample (n=35) drawn largely from a re-entry retreat. Percentages are directional signals, not statistical certainties. The full report names these limitations clearly. With those caveats noted, the findings are internally consistent and point strongly enough in certain directions to warrant serious attention.

Key findings

What the data tells us

01
The faith community gap is the most common wound
63% struggled to reconnect with their faith community — not theologically, but relationally. Missionaries come back changed. Most churches are not ready to receive that.
02
Emotional needs go unmet — often unrecognised
40% reported emotional or mental health struggles. Many only recognised the need months or years after return, triggered by secondary life events. Reactive support is not enough.
03
Financial pressure compounds everything else
37% cited financial strain. Singapore's housing costs and career gaps hit hard — particularly for those returning after a decade or more. The sending relationship tends to end the moment the missionary lands.
04
Families carry a compounded burden
For families, children's emotional adjustment, spousal wellbeing, and housing for larger households add layers that singles don't face. Support rarely extends meaningfully to spouses or children — even when they need it most.
05
Re-entry hits every area of life at once
It is not a housing problem, or a jobs problem, or a mental health problem. For most returnees, it is all of these simultaneously — and the weight of each compounds the others.

What made the difference

What actually helped

Re-entry retreats
Structured space to process, among people who understood. The simple act of being named and welcomed mattered deeply.
Close relationships
Friends who initiated without waiting to be asked — who listened without requiring explanation.
Financial buffers
Even a small transition allowance provided breathing room. Those without it described the financial pressure as consuming.
Church community
Not just the existence of a church — but specific people who took initiative and made intentional effort.

The support gap

What they wished they'd had

51%
Emotional & mental health support
Structured debriefs, trauma counselling, spiritual direction. Needs often surfaced months or years after return — not at the obvious moment of transition.
43%
Intentional church & community support
Not acknowledgement — intentionality. Someone taking responsibility for their reintegration, without immediately deploying them back into ministry.
34%
Peer connection with other returnees
The distinctiveness of re-entry is hard to communicate to those who haven't lived it. Peers provide something pastoral care from non-returnees cannot.
31%
Financial transition support
Not charity — a bridge. A short-season allowance to resettle without the compounding pressure of immediate employment urgency.
26%
Vocational & career support
Help translating field skills into terms local employers recognise. Particularly acute for those returning after 10+ years.

What this points toward

Possible ways forward

These are directional recommendations grounded in the survey findings — not prescriptions, but starting points for conversation.

Pastoral care
Proactive soul care — not reactive
Offer structured debriefing, counselling, and spiritual direction proactively — and keep it available for up to 2–3 years after return. Many struggles surface long after the obvious moment of transition.
Financial
A financial bridge at the point of return
A short-season transition allowance (SGD 2,000–4,000/month) gives returnees breathing room to resettle without compounding financial pressure on every other adjustment. Sending churches should treat this as a natural extension of their financial relationship — not a discretionary add-on.
Community
Build the peer network — not just a retreat
34% named peer connection as an unmet need. 54% are already mentoring others. The supply and demand exist — what's missing is the infrastructure to connect them in an ongoing rhythm, not just a one-off event.
Vocational
Skills translation for returning workers
Partner with career coaches and employers to help returnees articulate and apply their field skills. Priority for those returning after 10+ years, where vocational disorientation is most acute.
Families
Extend care to the whole family
Spouses, children, and non-Singaporean partners carry their own re-entry challenges — and currently receive almost no structured support. Family care must be explicit, not assumed.

A closing word

"God will not waste your pain. Returning back is a discipleship training. He is a good shepherd."

— Respondent, 11 years in East Asia

Every respondent who shared a hard story also shared evidence of resilience, faith, and ongoing fruitfulness. They are not a community in despair. They are a community waiting to be more fully seen, more intentionally supported, and more wisely deployed.

The same movement that built Singapore’s strong missionary-sending culture, is entirely capable of building the infrastructure to welcome its people home. With structured support in place, returned missionaries may not just survive re-entry, but become the most credible, experienced, and passionate advocates, mentors, and mobilisers for the next generation of goers.

"The same God who looked after you in the field will look after you when you return. Take lots of time and counsel to adjust."

— Respondent, 20 years in Southeast Asia
